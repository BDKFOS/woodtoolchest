Quick answer

A board foot is 144 cubic inches of lumber. The base formula is simple, but most woodworkers should buy more than the bare minimum. In real shops, the useful number is usually your calculated board footage plus waste, milling loss, and a little breathing room.

What the result means

How to use this tool well

  1. Start with your finished dimensions or your actual part sizes.
  2. Use the simple mode for quick math, the project mode for realistic shopping, and the real shop mode when rough lumber and milling losses are part of the job.
  3. Enter a price per board foot if you want a rough material cost before buying.
  4. Add enough allowance for defects, trimming, and grain choice; especially for visible furniture parts.
  5. When in doubt, buy a little extra. A second trip is usually more expensive than one extra board.

When woodworkers usually come up short

Ignoring milling loss

Rough lumber rarely ends at the same thickness and width you bought on paper.

Buying only exact footage

The formula does not account for defects, grain selection, or mistakes.

Forgetting trim waste

Crosscuts, kerf, and squaring ends quietly eat up material.

Simple rule: if the project is appearance-sensitive, uses rough stock, or needs matched parts, the true buy number is often 15% to 30% higher than the raw math.

FAQ

What is a board foot?

A board foot is a volume measurement equal to 144 cubic inches of lumber. One common way to picture it is 1 inch thick, 12 inches wide, and 12 inches long.

How do I calculate board feet from inches?

Multiply thickness by width by length, all in inches, then divide by 144.

Why does rough lumber need more allowance?

Because you usually lose some material to flattening, jointing, planing, trimming, cutting around defects, and selecting better grain.

How much extra lumber should I buy?

It depends on the project, but many woodworkers add around 10% to 20% for simpler work and more for figured stock, rough lumber, matched grain, or high-precision furniture parts.

Can I use this for cutting boards?

Yes. It is especially useful for cutting boards and glue-ups because those builds often need extra material for flattening, trimming, and dialing in a nicer final look.

Bottom line

The board foot formula is easy. Buying the right amount of wood is the hard part. Use the clean number as your baseline, then use the real-world estimate so your project survives milling, trimming, and the normal chaos of a woodworking shop.
