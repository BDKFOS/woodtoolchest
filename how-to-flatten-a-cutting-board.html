How to Flatten a Cutting Board

Flattening a cutting board is one of those jobs that sounds simple until the board is cupped, twisted, or just wide enough to make everything annoying. The right approach depends on the board, the warp, and the tools you actually have.

Quick answer

The safest and most flexible way to flatten a cutting board is with a router flattening jig. A planer works for some edge grain boards. Light unevenness can be cleaned up with sanding.

Best way to flatten a cutting board

For wide, warped, or end grain boards, use a router flattening jig. It gives control and avoids forcing stock through machines.

For mild edge grain cleanup, a planer can be faster. For very small imperfections, sanding may be enough.

Rule: More valuable board = more control, not more speed.

Edge grain vs end grain

Edge grain

Easier to flatten. Can sometimes go through a planer if already close to flat.

End grain

Needs more care. Router jig is usually the safer and more controlled option.

If you are unsure about planing a board, do not force it.

Using a router flattening jig

  • handles wide boards
  • controlled shallow passes
  • ideal for end grain
  • most reliable method overall

Shim the board so it cannot rock. Take shallow passes and mark the surface to track progress.

Using a planer

Works when:

  • edge grain
  • mostly flat already
  • fits comfortably

Not ideal when:

  • end grain
  • heavy warp
  • awkward size

Sanding only

Works for minor unevenness and cleanup, not major flattening.

  • small high spots
  • glue line cleanup
  • final finish prep

Step-by-step

  1. Check for warp
  2. Choose method
  3. Mark surface
  4. Flatten first face
  5. Flip and repeat
  6. Sand smooth
  7. Finish

Common mistakes

  • sanding major warp
  • forcing planer use
  • unstable jig setup
  • deep passes
  • rushing the process

FAQ

Best method for end grain?

Router jig.

Can I use a planer?

Sometimes, for edge grain only.

Do I still need sanding?

Yes.

Bottom line

Use the method that gives you control. For most real-world boards, that is a router flattening jig.
